Section A

Recognize Logistics Fundamentals

  • History, definitions, and scope

Section A • Page 2

History, Definitions, and Scope

  • Logistics has old military and trade roots, but modern business logistics became strategic when companies saw how tightly transport, warehousing, inventory, packaging, and order management interact.
  • The scope is broader than shipping. Logistics coordinates flow, storage, timing, information, and execution across fulfillment.
  • Great logistics helps meet demand at the right place, time, and cost.

Say it simply

Logistics is how an organization gets the right thing to the right place at the right time with the right support.

Section A • Page 3

Total Cost and Tradeoffs

  • The total cost concept means choosing the option with the best total logistics result, not the cheapest isolated function.
  • Cost buckets include local delivery, primary transport, distribution center, inventory carrying, information system, and additional system costs.
  • A tradeoff happens when one improvement creates a cost or service downside somewhere else.

Tap for a fast example

A slower shipping mode may cut freight cost, but it can force higher inventory and hurt service responsiveness.

Section B

Role, Value, and Cost of Logistics

  • Supply chain management is broader than logistics. It includes network design, planning, execution, control, and synchronization across the whole chain.
  • Logistics remains a core capability inside SCM because it executes and coordinates physical and information flows.
  • Value shows up in service, resilience, customer satisfaction, competitiveness, and disciplined cost management.

Maturity ladder

Multiple dysfunction -> Semifunctional enterprise -> Integrated enterprise -> Extended enterprise -> Orchestrated supply chain.

Section B • Page 2

How Logistics Creates Value

  • Better logistics can improve customer experience and increase competitive advantage.
  • Strong logistics leadership also manages transportation, labor, and inventory costs with a wider business lens.
  • Module 1 also links logistics to resilience and sustainability, not just cost cutting.

Achievement cue

If you can explain why logistics matters beyond shipping cost, you understand the heart of Section B.

Section C

Develop Logistics Strategy

  • Logistics strategy should align with company and supply chain goals.
  • Module 1 emphasizes strategic, tactical, and operational alignment.
  • A logistics value proposition is the promise of value delivered to a target customer or stakeholder group.

Three strategy lenses: process, market, and information.

Section C • Page 2

EERS and Design Choices

  • EERS stands for effective, efficient, relevant, and sustainable.
  • Optimization requires systems thinking across warehousing, inventory, transportation, labor, and technology.
  • Segmentation, contracting, and product life cycle stage can all change the right logistics design.

Memory shortcut

If a strategy is cheap but not relevant or sustainable, Module 1 would not call it strong enough.
